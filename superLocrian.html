Super Locrian

Nice choice...
Having names for things can be useful, but sometimes those names just aren’t so informative - what’s more locrian than locrian? super locrian?

A more descriptive name for this scale is the Altered Dominant Scale, the reasoning for this requires some brief context, bear with me.

....it’s easy enough to accept that for the most part, the most informative notes of a 4 note chord built using the 1st, 3rd, 5th and 7th degrees of the scale are the 1, 3, and 7, as the interval of the 5th degree is the same in all major 7, minor 7 and dominant 7 chords.

We can call super locrian the altered dominant scale because while it retains the essential notes of a dominant 7 chord, (1st, 3rd, & b7th degrees), all other tones are “altered".

If we were to number the scale tones conventionally, i.e. strictly 1-7, we can see why some folks call this scale Super Locrian - it is essentially locrian with a "b4". Some folks though argue you can't have a "b4", it must be called a "3". Whatever you want to call it, it can be used to create some colorful tension over a dominant chord. Experiment..

Super Locrian / the Altered Dominant scale actually occurs (or may be derived) when you begin the melodic minor scale from its 7th degree, thus a third name for it is simply the 7th mode of melodic minor. Sorry to have gone through so much trouble.
